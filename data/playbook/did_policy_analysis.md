# Difference-in-Differences and Policy Analysis

## When to Use DiD

Use difference-in-differences or another quasi-experimental method when a change was not randomized but there is a plausible comparison group and pre/post data. Examples include a feature launched in one city, a policy changed in one market, or a ranking rule enabled for one seller category.

## Why Simple Pre/Post Is Risky

Simple before/after comparisons can be confounded by seasonality, marketing campaigns, economic shocks, holidays, competitor changes, and user mix shifts. A simple comparison between treated and untreated markets can also be biased if the markets differ before treatment.

## DiD Requirements

Difference-in-differences needs a treated group, a comparison group, pre-treatment outcomes, post-treatment outcomes, and a credible parallel-trends assumption. Always inspect pre-trends before trusting the estimate.

## Launch Guidance

When the rollout is non-random, do not describe the result as an A/B test. Use quasi-experimental language, show pre-trend diagnostics, include sensitivity checks, and be more conservative about launch claims.
